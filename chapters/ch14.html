FOUNDATIONS

The TMT Lexicon

ARR / MRR
Annual / Monthly Recurring Revenue. The heartbeat of SaaS — predictable, contracted revenue.
NRR (Net Revenue Retention)
Retained revenue from existing customers plus expansion revenue, minus churn. Above 100% signals strong product-market fit.
LTV / CAC
Lifetime Value divided by Customer Acquisition Cost. Gold standard unit economics. Target: 3.0x or higher.
DAU / MAU
Daily / Monthly Active Users. Engagement metric for consumer platforms. Ratio indicates "stickiness."
GMV
Gross Merchandise Value. Total transaction volume in a marketplace. Not the same as revenue.
Take Rate
Marketplace revenue as a percentage of GMV. Higher take rates = better unit economics.
ARPU / ARPPU
Average Revenue Per User / Per Paying User. Monetization intensity across cohorts.
Churn Rate
Percentage of customers lost per period. Lower is better. In SaaS, watch both dollar churn and logo churn.
CORE MODEL

B2B SaaS

Revenue Mechanics: Subscription-based, recurring, monthly or annual contracts. Expansion revenue comes from upsell, cross-sell, and seat growth. The predictability of recurring revenue is the core defensibility.

Key Metrics:

  • ARR: The north star. Watch growth rate and cohort expansion.
  • NRR: Above 100% means existing customer revenue is growing. Gold standard.
  • Rule of 40: Growth Rate + EBITDA Margin ≥ 40%. Healthy SaaS balances growth and profitability.

Unit Economics: LTV should be 3–5x CAC. CAC payback period (time to recover acquisition cost) should be under 12 months. Watch for hidden customer success costs that inflate true CAC.

Defensibility: Switching costs (data lock-in, integrations, training), high employee adoption (makes switching painful), and multi-product stickiness.

INFRASTRUCTURE

Cloud & Vertical SaaS

Cloud Infrastructure (IaaS/PaaS): Consumption-based pricing model — customers pay for compute, storage, or API calls. High capital requirements to build and maintain data centers. Tends toward winner-take-most (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) because of switching costs and network effects in the ecosystem.

Key Dynamics:

  • Capex-heavy; difficult to reverse.
  • Margin expansion through scale and efficiency gains.
  • Vendor lock-in: customers' entire infrastructure depends on the platform.
  • Pricing power increases as the platform becomes indispensable.

Vertical SaaS: Deep focus on a single industry or use case (e.g., SaaS for dental practices, legal firms, construction). Higher NRR than horizontal SaaS because products are more tightly integrated with niche workflows. Narrower TAM but higher penetration and switching costs.

Embedded Payments: Many vertical SaaS companies embed payment processing — adding another revenue lever (take rate on transactions) and stickiness.

HYBRID MODELS

Embedded Finance & AdTech

Embedded Finance: Financial services rails embedded inside non-financial products. Examples: Shopify Payments, Buy Now Pay Later, marketplace lending. Revenue comes from transaction fees, interchange, or spread.

BaaS (Banking-as-a-Service): White-label banking infrastructure. Companies license banking licenses and regulatory compliance, allowing fintech startups to offer deposit, payment, and lending services without becoming a bank.

Key Risks: Regulatory scrutiny, fraud exposure, capital requirements for lending products, and the challenge of compliance at scale.

AdTech & MarTech: Programmatic advertising, demand-side platforms (DSPs), supply-side platforms (SSPs), data brokers. Revenue model: CPM (cost per mille/thousand impressions), CPC (cost per click), CPA (cost per action).

Diligence Focus: Attribution accuracy, viewability rates, brand safety, regulatory pressure on cookies and data, and margin compression from competition.

MARKETPLACE ECONOMICS

GMV Is Not Revenue

The Chain: GMV (total transacted) → × Take Rate = Net Revenue → − Costs = Gross Profit

Example: $100B GMV marketplace with 2% take rate generates $2B in revenue. Then subtract costs (platform, customer support, payment processing, hosting) to get actual gross profit.

Common Pitfalls:

  • GMV-as-revenue: Using GMV multiples instead of revenue multiples. Dangerous. A $10B GMV marketplace valued at 3x GMV sounds cheap until you realize it's generating only $200M revenue (2% take) and valued at 150x revenue.
  • Overstated cohort performance: Showing gross bookings instead of net revenue; not accounting for cancellations, refunds, and payment failures.
  • Gross vs. net confusion: Mixing GMV with revenue in the same breath. Always separate them.
  • Take rate decay: As marketplaces mature and competition increases, take rates compress.

Diligence: Always derive revenue from GMV and stated take rate. Verify take rate trends over time.

RETENTION DYNAMICS

Network Effects & Non-Contractual Retention

Types of Network Effects:

  • Direct: Value increases with each user (e.g., Slack, WhatsApp). Rare and powerful.
  • Cross-side: Buyers benefit from more sellers, and vice versa (marketplaces). Often weaker than direct.
  • Data-driven: Platform gets smarter with scale, improving user experience (e.g., search, recommendation engines).

Contractual vs. Non-Contractual Retention: SaaS has contractual lock-in (signed agreements). Marketplaces and consumer apps rely on non-contractual retention — users stay because they prefer the platform, not because they're bound.

Churn Ladder: Understanding how users churn in stages: from active → inactive → dormant → deleted. Multi-homing risk: users' willingness to use competitors simultaneously erodes switching costs.

Key Metrics: 30-day retention (% of Day 0 users still active 30 days later), DAU/MAU ratio (engagement stickiness), and cohort retention curves.

PLATFORM MODELS

Marketplaces

Core Dynamics: Two-sided networks with distinct buyer and seller economics. Success requires solving the chicken-and-egg problem — bootstrap both sides simultaneously without either subsidizing the other perpetually.

Take Rate & Trust: Higher take rates incentivize the platform but can push sellers to multi-home or transact off-platform. Trust mechanisms (reviews, ratings, fraud protection, insurance) are essential but costly.

Horizontal vs. Vertical: Horizontal marketplaces (Etsy, eBay) are harder to scale but have larger TAM. Vertical marketplaces (industry-specific) have lower TAM but higher attachment and switching costs.

Disintermediation Risk: Sellers and buyers may connect directly, bypassing the platform and its take rate. More acute in B2B or services marketplaces.

CAC Payback in Marketplaces: Unlike SaaS's monthly recurring model, marketplaces' unit economics depend on repeat transaction frequency and take rate. CAC payback can take 12–24 months or longer.

Scale Strategy: Successful marketplaces often use subsidies (discounts, bounties) to bootstrap one side, then rely on network effects to reduce subsidy dependency.

CONSUMER TECH

Consumer Platforms & Superapps

Engagement Loops: Consumer platforms live or die by habit-forming engagement. Key levers:

  • Social graph: Friends' activity drives return visits (Facebook, Instagram).
  • Content discovery: Algorithmic recommendations keep users scrolling (TikTok, YouTube).
  • Gamification: Points, streaks, leaderboards, achievement badges.
  • Notifications: Timely alerts to re-engage lapsed users (often overused and damaging to retention).

Monetization Paths: Ads (CPM-based), in-app purchases (IAP, virtual goods), subscriptions (premium tier), and transactions (take rate on bookings or payments).

Superapps (WeChat, Grab, Alipay model): One app, multiple services. Ride-hailing + payments + shopping + social. ARPU is higher because of multiple revenue streams and cross-selling opportunities. Requires massive scale to justify the engineering complexity.

Key Metrics: DAU/MAU (daily divided by monthly actives — higher is stickier), retention cohorts at day 1, 7, 30, and 90, and ARPU/ARPPU growth.

EDGE MONETISATION

Creator Economies

Platform Rake: The platform takes a commission on creator earnings. Examples:

  • YouTube: 55% to creator, 45% to YouTube (but varies by geography and ad type).
  • App Store: 70% to developer, 30% to Apple (plus tax).
  • Patreon: 5–12% platform fee depending on tier.
  • Twitch: 50/50 split on subscriptions; variable on ads.

Creator Concentration Risk: A small percentage of creators generate the majority of revenue. Losing top creators can crater platform revenue and reputation. Platform dependence is high; creators are always threatened by rival platforms offering better terms.

Monetization Levers: Ads (CPM-based), subscriptions (recurring access to exclusive content), tips/donations, virtual goods (emotes, badges), and revenue sharing on creator-adjacent services.

Diligence: Track creator churn, average creator tenure, and concentration metrics (e.g., top 1% of creators' share of revenue). Watch for top creators threatening to leave.

STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS

Niche Models & Their Weaknesses

Social-Graph Platforms (Facebook, Snapchat, TikTok): Saturation in mature markets (growth slowing), content fatigue (users running out of novel content to consume), and privacy regulation (GDPR, iOS App Tracking Transparency) eroding targeting and attribution.

Zero-to-One / Hyperlocal Platforms (local services, delivery): Multi-homing (users and providers use multiple apps), thin margins (high take rate → low supply incentive), regulatory risk (gig classification, price controls), and difficulty achieving unit economics.

Hardware-Backed Software (Apple Watch, VR headsets, gaming consoles): Lower software margins due to hardware subsidy; product cycles are longer and riskier; ecosystem lock-in is strong but fragile (if hardware fails, the ecosystem collapses). Obsolescence cycles are unforgiving.

Creator-Dependent Platforms (OnlyFans, Substack): Creator churn is devastating. Platform lock-in is weak (creators can email followers directly). Revenue per creator is often insufficient, leading to burnout and migration.

DUE DILIGENCE

Red Flags in TMT

Inflated Take Rates: Stated take rate is higher than industry benchmarks. Either the company is unsustainably undercutting competitors or the stated rate is inflated (users are transacting off-platform).

Artificial GMV: GMV includes canceled orders, returned goods, or intercompany transactions. Always separate GMV into completed transactions and actual customer revenue.

Gross vs. Net Confusion: Company mixes GMV with revenue in earnings presentations. Watch for it in guidance and press releases.

CAC Delusions:

  • Ignoring true CAC costs (customer success, onboarding, refunds, payment processing).
  • Unrealistic LTV assumptions (assuming customers stay forever at premium pricing).
  • Blended cohorts (mixing high-quality and low-quality cohorts to hide bad retention in some segments).
  • No cohort breakdown by channel (organic vs. paid), geography, or customer segment.

Selective Cohort Reporting: Company highlights strong cohorts but omits weaker ones. Demand full cohort detail across all acquisition channels.

Over-Adjusted EBITDA: Too many adjustments hide operating reality. Scrutinize every adjustment; reconstruct EBITDA from cash flow.

Misaligned Incentives: Leadership is overcompensated relative to performance; stock grants are excessive; employee turnover is high.
